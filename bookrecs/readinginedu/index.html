Reading In Education

Most people go through a book phase in middle school but drop it sometime in early high school when books become assignments rather than fun. Instead of lenses into new worlds, books become a waste of time, a hassle preventing us from what we really want to do. I went from reading 3-4 hours per day in middle school to, within a year, replacing high school reading assignments with Sparknotes binges before class. Not all of these were “boring” books either—I would’ve loved The Kite Runner had I read it willingly. I’ve heard the same story that I experienced from so many friends. I was lucky enough in college to have a few weeks of motivation and some great books to get back into reading as a hobby.

I believe the problem is that we teach reading in the same way that we teach math and science. It is treated as a formulaic task of memorization rather than a creative journey. The moment when we are taught to annotate books for a certain theme or literary device is the moment when the fun and imagination dissipates from the book for most people. I remember dashing through great books such as To Kill a Mockingbird scanning for similes, motifs, and characterization to highlight instead of actually enjoying the material and stepping into Harper Lee’s 1930s with the Finches. While it may be true that teenagers refuse everything forced upon them, the education system rewards memorization and knowledge rather than genuine interest and love for the craft. It is understandable that students are forced to learn biology and calculus, but drilling into students the art of writing a 5 page essay on the significance of personification in a book is self-defeating. Almost everyone I know has come out of high school English class with worse reading habits than going in. After basic literacy, reading should be encouraged as a playground where students can explore and formulate their own thoughts and opinions. It should be an art like painting or orchestra, not a ‘hard subject’ like calculus.
